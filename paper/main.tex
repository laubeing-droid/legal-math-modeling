\begin{abstract}
We present a formal mathematical framework for symbolic legal reasoning across
multiple jurisdictions (PRC, Hong Kong, United States). The framework consists
of a three-layer legal ontology ($L_0$/$L_1$/$L_2$), a monotone Horn closure
engine for forward fact expansion, a Dung abstract argumentation framework for
rebuttal and exception handling, Kripke temporal models for procedural state
tracking, and category-theoretic mappings for cross-jurisdictional concept
alignment.

The formal core comprises 43 machine-checked theorems in Lean~4 (0 sorry,
0 custom axiom) spanning three layers: a finite monotone iteration kernel,
a Dung grounded extension fixed-point layer, and a finite Horn closure layer.
An additional 57 supporting results extend the checked surface to 100 total
theorems. The kernel is shared: both the AAF and Horn tracks instantiate the
same finite monotone iteration theorem.

We also establish several negative results: the unified evaluator is
non-monotone (requiring stratification), differential privacy epsilon is not
determined by privilege level, legal case similarity is not a metric, and no
total cross-jurisdiction functor exists (obstruction density $> 2/3$ over
44 real claim mappings).

All mathematical claims are tracked by a 7-level
\emph{evidence-calibrated trust label system} that prevents unverified
assertions from propagating to downstream engineering decisions. The boundary
between Lean-proven properties and engineering-verified (Python) properties is
explicitly documented.

The framework is implemented as a runnable Python library (59 modules) with
Lean~4 formal proofs, Z3 SMT verification, and Hypothesis property-based
testing. The current status is \texttt{spec-first-transition-ready}: the
mathematical specification is sufficient for the \texttt{juris-calculus}
runtime to proceed with a unique upstream semantic source.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} computational law, formal methods, legal AI,
abstract argumentation, Horn logic, Kripsemantics, category theory,
evidence calibration, finite monotone iteration
\end{abstract}
