\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Legal reasoning across jurisdictions presents a unique formal challenge:
concepts that are well-defined in one legal system (e.g., ``material breach''
in U.S. contract law) have no direct equivalent in another (e.g., Chinese
contract law under the Civil Code). Existing computational legal systems
typically rely on probabilistic retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), which
lacks formal guarantees about soundness, completeness, or cross-jurisdictional
consistency.

This paper presents \textbf{juris-calculus}, a symbolic legal reasoning engine
that operates across three jurisdictions---the People's Republic of China
(PRC), Hong Kong (HK), and the United States (US)---using deterministic
fixpoint evaluation rather than probabilistic language models. The core
contribution is a \emph{mathematical framework} that formalizes:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item A \textbf{three-layer legal ontology} ($L_0$/$L_1$/$L_2$) with 6
        irreducible primitives, 15 meta-ontological categories, and 20+
        jurisdiction-specific domain concepts (Section~\ref{sec:ontology});
  \item A \textbf{monotone Horn closure} engine for forward fact expansion
        over 2,117 PRC rules, with proved correctness under bounded depth
        $k \leq 3$ (Section~\ref{sec:horn});
  \item A \textbf{Dung abstract argumentation framework} for rebuttal and
        exception handling, with exhaustive verification for $n \leq 4$
        (Section~\ref{sec:aaf});
  \item \textbf{Kripke temporal models} with LTL embedding for procedural
        state tracking (Section~\ref{sec:kripke});
  \item \textbf{Category-theoretic} and \textbf{Banach contraction} models
        for cross-jurisdictional mapping and pricing, with explicit
        counterexamples marking their boundaries
        (Sections~\ref{sec:category}--\ref{sec:banach});
  \item A 7-level \textbf{evidence-calibrated trust label system} that
        tracks every mathematical claim through its proof lifecycle
        (Section~\ref{sec:trust}).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Research Methodology: AI-Assisted Formalization}

This work employs an iterative AI-assisted methodology:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Claude} (Anthropic) performed initial mathematical modeling
        and reverse engineering of the legal reasoning architecture;
  \item \textbf{Codex}~\cite{codex2026} (OpenAI) conducted formal verification using 7 tool
        chains: Hypothesis (property-based testing), Z3~\cite{z3} (SMT solving),
        CrossHair (contract verification), TLA+ (model checking), Alloy
        (relational analysis), Lean~4~\cite{lean4} (theorem proving), and Dafny
        (program verification);
  \item \textbf{Kimi} (Moonshot AI) collected legal validation data and
        produced a strict mathematical proof rework;
  \item A second \textbf{Codex} audit downgraded overstated claims and
        established the final evidence-calibrated baseline.
\end{enumerate}

Each AI agent contributed a different verification modality. The trust label
system (Section~\ref{sec:trust}) records which agent produced which evidence,
preventing any single agent's output from being promoted beyond its verified
scope.
