\subsection{Refuted Claims}

The following claims were \textbf{refuted} by explicit counterexamples:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Evaluator monotonicity}: The original combined evaluator
        is non-monotone (Counterexample~\ref{ce:eval-mono}).
  \item \textbf{DP $\varepsilon$ determinability}: No monotone
        $\varepsilon$ function exists over the CN privilege lattice
        (Theorem~\ref{thm:dp-cn}).
  \item \textbf{Cross-graph monotonicity}: Adding a fact can shrink the
        grounded extension (Counterexample~\ref{ce:stage3b}).
  \item \textbf{Floor-clipping DP}: The mechanism violates
        $\varepsilon$-DP (Counterexample~\ref{ce:dp-floor}).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Data-Insufficient and Planned Results}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Full-dimensional Banach contraction}:
        \texttt{BanachEffectiveNodes.lean} proves the contraction for
        the finite effective-node pricing function only. The
        multi-dimensional case requires a metric on the full pricing
        space and real timesheet data (currently: 0 real timesheets,
        225 fee-schedule proxies).

  \item \textbf{Real-data Rosetta functor}:
        \texttt{FiniteRosetta.lean} proves \texttt{no\_total\_functor}
        for the finite toy model ($\texttt{obstruction} = 37$). The
        full legal inventory ($> 2{,}000$ concepts) requires a dataset
        of $\geq 2{,}000$ cross-jurisdiction concept pairs.

  \item \textbf{General AAF convergence for $n > 4$}:
        \texttt{DungFixedPoint.lean} proves the grounded extension
        exists and is unique for all finite AFs (by the
        \texttt{FiniteMonotoneIteration} theorem). The \emph{operational}
        convergence bound (number of iterations) for $n > 4$ remains
        to be characterized.

  \item \textbf{Full evaluator equivalence}:
        Does the stratified evaluator (Horn then AAF) produce identical
        output to the original combined evaluator for all production
        rules? This is the most important open question for the
        juris-calculus architecture.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Spec-First Transition Readiness}

The juris-calculus system is designed for a \emph{spec-first} transition
from formal specification to production implementation:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Formal spec}: 64 Lean theorems specify the behavior of
        each component (Horn closure, AAF, pricing, temporal model);
  \item \textbf{Trust labels}: The 7-level evidence system
        (Section~\ref{sec:trust}) gates every engineering decision on
        its verified evidence level;
  \item \textbf{Certificate/checker boundary}: Lean proofs are checked
        by the Lean kernel, Z3 scripts by Z3's proof checker, Python
        enumerations by the test runner. No claim crosses its checker
        boundary;
  \item \textbf{TAINTED fail-closed}: Refuted or unverifiable facts
        are excluded from outputs.
\end{enumerate}

This architecture ensures that the gap between ``formally specified''
and ``production-ready'' is explicitly tracked and never silently closed.

\subsection{Open Problems}

\begin{openproblem}[Full evaluator equivalence]
\label{op:eval-equiv}
Does the stratified evaluator produce identical output to the original
combined evaluator for all production rules? The cross-graph monotonicity
failure (Counterexample~\ref{ce:stage3b}) suggests this may require a
more refined analysis than simple monotone iteration.
\end{openproblem}

\begin{openproblem}[Real-data Rosetta functor]
\label{op:rosetta-real}
Does a total semantic-preserving functor exist for the complete PRC/HK/US
legal inventory? The current finite model shows
$\texttt{obstruction\_density} > 2/3$.
\end{openproblem}

\begin{openproblem}[Full-dimensional Banach pricing]
\label{op:banach-full}
Can the pricing map be extended to a full-dimensional Banach contraction
with a well-defined metric and real timesheet data?
\end{openproblem}

\begin{openproblem}[Lean formalization completion]
\label{op:lean}
Several Lean files contain theorems proved without \texttt{sorry}.
Extending the formalization to cover the data-insufficient claims
(Rosetta real, Banach full) would elevate their trust labels from
\textsc{Data\_Insufficient} to \textsc{Lean\_Proved}.
\end{openproblem}

\subsection{Future Work}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Data collection}: Acquire $\geq 2{,}000$ real
        cross-jurisdiction concept pairs and $\geq 100$ real timesheets
        to resolve the data-insufficient claims;
  \item \textbf{Lean Mathlib integration}: Port the
        \texttt{WeightedSupNorm} and \texttt{FiniteRosetta} results
        to Mathlib-compatible definitions for broader reuse;
  \item \textbf{Runtime monitoring}: Implement the trust label system
        as a runtime gate in the juris-calculus evaluation engine;
  \item \textbf{Extended AAF}: Characterize the operational convergence
        bound for Dung AFs with $n > 4$ arguments;
  \item \textbf{Cross-system validation}: Apply the evidence-calibrated
        trust label methodology to other AI-assisted formalization
        projects (drug discovery, financial modeling).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Broader Impact}

The evidence-calibrated trust label system is applicable beyond legal
reasoning. Any domain where AI agents produce mathematical claims can
adopt this system to prevent the ``hallucination-to-production'' pipeline
where unverified AI assertions become engineering invariants.

The key principle is simple: \textbf{the strongest claim you can make is
the one your evidence supports---and no stronger.}
